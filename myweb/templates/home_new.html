Design and Construction of a low power Audio Amplifier using LM386

I led a team of four members in a mini project to design and construct a low-power audio amplifier using the LM386 integrated circuit (IC).

The Initial prototype was assembled on a breadboard, and after successful testing, it was permanently soldered onto a Vero board for final packaging.

To test the amplifier, an audio jack was connected from a phone with a music player to the amplifier's input, resulting in amplified audio output. A 10K potentiometer was used a both a switch and volume control. You can read more about a similar project here

Design and Construction of Up and Down Decade Counter using JK Flip Flop

I led a team of four members in another group project to design and construct an up/down decade counter using JK flip-flops.

To achieve counting from 0 to 9 and 9 to 0, we utilized four JK flip-flops. The procedures followed included constructing the state transition table, determining the J and K inputs for each of the four JK flip-flops using their characteristic tables and karnaugh maps, and simulating the resulting circuit using Proteus prototyping software. Hardware implementation and construction were carried out after successful simulation. Read more about JK flip-flops here

Design and Construction of a GUI based Wireless Home Appliance Switching system using Matlab

The project employs a set of buttons designed using MATLAB to control the switching of remote home appliances via GSM technology. The design is divided into two main units: the transmitting unit and the receiving unit.

The transmitting unit consists of a graphical user interface (GUI) implemented with MATLAB, a USB-to-TTL converter, and a GSM module that functions as the wireless transmitter. The GUI buttons are programmed to send commands through the GSM module to the remote home appliances when toggled. Depending on the command issued, the target appliance is either switched on or off.

The receiving unit includes another GSM module to receive the transmitted commands, a microcontroller (Arduino Nano) to process these commands, and relay modules to execute the switching. The microcontroller interprets the received commands and toggles the state of its digital output pins accordingly. These pins are connected to the relay modules, which then make or break the circuit, thereby controlling the power state of the appliances connected to their high-voltage terminals.
